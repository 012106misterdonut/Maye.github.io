TECHNOLOGY TIMELINE TO COMPUTING'S IMPACT TO INDUSTRIES


See more

THE EVOLUTION OF COMPUTING



MECHANICAL ERA (1642-1934)
This era saw the development of early mechanical calculating machines like the abacus, Pascaline, and Babbage's Difference Engine. These devices laid the foundation for modern computers by demonstrating the potential for automating calculations.

VACUUM TUBES ERA (1935-1946)
The invention of the vacuum tube led to the creation of the first electronic computers, such as Colossus and ENIAC. These machines were significantly faster and more powerful than their mechanical predecessors.

TRANSISTORS ERA (1947-1962)
The development of the transistor revolutionized computing. Transistors were smaller, more reliable, and consumed less power than vacuum tubes. This era saw the emergence of programming languages like FORTRAN, ALGOL, and COBOL, as well as the first supercomputers.

INTEGRATED CIRCUIT ERA (1963-1972)
The invention of the integrated circuit (IC) allowed for the miniaturization of electronic components, leading to smaller, more powerful, and more affordable computers. This era also saw the rise of operating systems and time-sharing.

PC & VLSI ERA (1972-Present)
The development of Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) allowed for the creation of microprocessors, which could fit entire computers on a single chip. This led to the personal computer revolution, with the introduction of devices like the Macintosh and the widespread adoption of computers in society.


COMPUTING'S' IMPACT ON SOCIETY AND INDUSTRIES



Computing has had a profound impact on virtually every aspect of modern life. Here are some key examples:

Education- Computers have revolutionized education, providing tools for computer-based education (CBE), student database management, and personalized learning experiences.

Business- Computers are essential for business operations, enabling tasks like payroll calculations, budgeting, sales analysis, and financial forecasting.

Marketing- Computers have transformed marketing, allowing for the creation of digital advertising, online shopping, and targeted marketing campaigns.

Military- Computers are integral to modern warfare, used for missile control, military communication, operation planning, and the development of smart weapons.

Healthcare- Computers play a vital role in healthcare, enabling diagnostic systems, lab testing, patient monitoring, and even surgical procedures.

Communication- Computers have revolutionized communication, facilitating email, instant messaging, video conferencing, and the global exchange of information.

DATA vs. INFORMATION


Data

Raw, unprocessed facts, figures, and symbols that lack context or meaning.

Information

Processed and organized data that provides context and meaning, enabling informed decisions and actions.

Data Processing

The systematic manipulation of data to convert it into a more useful form. This involves a series of steps to transform raw data into meaningful insights or output.

Information Processing Cycle

A sequence of events consisting of



4 PHASES OF THE CYCLE TO PROCESS INFORMATION


Input- Acquiring, entering, and validating data.

Processing- Applying instructions to data to produce information.

Storage- Saving information for future use.

Output- Presenting information to users in a usable format.




Characteristics of Good Information


RELEVANT- Information should be important and useful to the recipient.

COMPLETE- No vital information should be omitted.

TIMELY- Information must be available when needed.

ACCURATE- Information must be correct and reliable.

COST-EFFECTIVE- Only essential data is necessary for sound decision-making.

PRESENTABLE- Good presentation makes information more understandable.

4 COMPONENTS IN A COMPUTER SYSTEM





Hardware



The physical components of a computer system, including:

1. Input Devices

— Devices used to enter data into the computer system.

2. Output Devices

— Devices used to present processed information to users.

3. Storage Devices

4. Memory


— Devices used to store data and programs.

1. Primary Memory — A type of computer memory that a processor accesses directly and quickly. It's used for temporarily storing applications and services currently in use. It's volatile, meaning data is lost when power is off.

Examples:
Random Access Memory (RAM)
Read Only Memory (ROM)
Programmable Read Only Memory (PROM)
Erasable Programmable Read Only Memory (EPROM)
Cache Memory

2. Secondary Memory — Also known as external memory or non-volatile memory. It's slower than primary memory but stores data permanently, even when the power is off.

Examples:
CD-ROM
DVD
Magnetic Tape
Magnetic Disk
Floppy Disk
Optical Disk

5. Processors

— The brain of the computer, responsible for executing instructions and performing calculations.

6. Scanning Devices

— Devices used to capture images or documents.

Software


A collection of electronic instructions that programmers write using a programming language and that a computer's CPU can interpret to carry out a specific task.

Two main types of software are:

1. System Software — Programs that manage the computer's resources, including the operating system, drivers, and utilities.

Classification of System Software:

MULTI-USER: Allows multiple users to run programs concurrently.

MULTI-PROCESSING: Enables a program to utilize multiple CPUs.

MULTI-TASKING: Allows more than one program to run simultaneously.

MULTI-THREADING: Permits different parts of a single program to run concurrently.

REAL-TIME: Responds to input immediately.

2. Application Software — Application software encompasses all programs designed to perform specific tasks for users.

A. Business Application: Software tailored for business use, often pre-packaged (canned) and designed according to business principles. Examples include word processors and spreadsheets.
B. Personal Application: Software designed to simplify or enhance personal tasks, often adding a fun element. Examples include email clients, reminders, and phone books.
C. Entertainment Application: Software focused on entertainment, but often with embedded educational objectives. Examples include computer games, media players, and movie apps.
D. Utility Application: Software that helps manage and maintain the computer itself. Examples include disk defragmenters and antivirus progra

Peopleware


The people who work with and use computer systems, including:

SYSTEM DESIGNERS: Individuals who design and develop computer systems.

SYSTEM ANALYSTS: Individuals who analyze user needs and translate them into system requirements.

PROGRAMMERS: Individuals who write the code for computer programs.

SYSTEM ADMINISTRATORS: Individuals who manage and maintain computer systems.

END USERS: Individuals who use computer systems to perform their tasks.

Dataware


The data that is stored, organized, manipulated, and analyzed by computer systems. This includes DATABASES, DATA WAREHOUSES, and DATA ANALYTICS TOOLS.

Difference between Data and Information


Data — Raw, unprocessed facts, figures, and symbols that lack context or meaning.

Information — Processed and organized data that provides context and meaning, enabling informed decisions and actions.

Example of Data vs. Information


Data

UT, 1234, Joe, Circle, SLC, 8015553211, 84084, Smith

Information

Joe Smith, 1234 Circle, Salt Lake City, UT 84084, (801) 555-3211

BASIC CONCEPT OF COMPUTER SECURITY


See more

DATA SECURITY




Classifying Information


PUBLIC — Information that can be freely shared with anyone. No restrictions apply.

INTERNAL — Potentially sensitive information that should not be shared outside the organization. This information is for internal use only.

CONFIDENTIAL — Information that may adversely affect employees, individuals, or the business if disclosed to unauthorized parties. This requires a higher level of protection.

RESTRICTED — Information subject to regulatory or legal obligations to maintain and protect. This is the most sensitive category, often requiring strict access controls and security measures.


Common Types of Cybercrimes


Identity Theft


Using fake credentials or malware to steal identities for financial gain.

Malicious Software


Software designed to harm computers, steal data, or cause disruption.

Theft


Includes piracy, illegal downloading, and unauthorized access to systems.

Cyberbullying and Stalking


Harassment and intimidation using digital devices, with cyberstalking representing a more serious form.

Child Soliciting and Abuse


Adults exploiting minors online for sexual or harmful purposes.

Hacking


Unauthorized access to computer systems or networks.


Types of Hackers


White Hats: Ethical hackers who test security systems.
Black Hats: Malicious hackers who breach systems for personal gain.
Grey Hats: Hackers who sometimes find vulnerabilities and publish them without malicious intent.
Hacktivists: Hackers with political motives.

Red Hat Hackers: Focus on Linux systems.

Scamming and Phishing


Deceptive tactics to trick users into revealing sensitive information.

Insider


Individuals with authorized access who misuse it for malicious purposes.


Cyber-Attacks Countermeasures


Network Encryption

Scrambles data as it travels over networks, protecting it from interception.

Proxies

Intermediaries that provide privacy, access to blocked content, and improved network performance.

Firewalls

Digital barriers that protect networks from unwanted traffic.

Cyber Liability Insurance

Financial protection against cyber-related incidents.

WHAT IS INTERNET?


The Internet, or simply the Net, is a global network of interconnected computer networks. It transmits data using packet switching and standardized protocols like IP. It consists of thousands of smaller networks (commercial, academic, government). It carries information and services like email, online chat, and the World Wide Web. It is the largest and most extensive internet in the world.



The Internet vs. the World Wide Web

While people often use "Internet" to refer to the "World Wide Web," they are not the same. The Internet encompasses various services, including:



  • World Wide Web (WWW)
  • Email
  • Chat rooms (Internet Relay Chat)
  • Message boards
  • Listservs
  • Usenet newsgroups
  • Telnet
  • File Transfer Protocol (FTP)


Cloud Computing


The Internet's ubiquity allows for constant, flexible access to information and services from anywhere. Cloud computing enables remote data storage and retrieval, eliminating the need for physical storage on individual devices. It benefits businesses by:


  • Eliminating the need for in-house data centers.
  • Handling data storage, processing, and security.


Cloud solutions are implemented through:


  • Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) - access applications
  • Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS) - build custom applications
  • Infrastructure-as-a-Service (IaaS) - large-scale infrastructure needs (Amazon, Google, Microsoft)


Machine Learning & Neural Networks


Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that enables systems to learn from data. It identifies patterns and makes decisions with minimal human intervention. Machine learning models improve and adapt over time by analyzing data.

Applications:


  • Business - predictive analytics and customer insights
  • Healthcare - disease diagnosis and personalized treatment
  • Finance - fraud detection and investment forecasting
  • Streaming services - recommendation algorithms
  • Autonomous vehicles


Neural networks are a core technology in machine learning. They are inspired by the structure of the human brain. They consist of layers of interconnected nodes ("neurons") that process and analyze data. Neural networks are powerful for tasks like:


  • Image and speech recognition
  • Language translation
  • Recommendation systems


The Internet of Things (IoT)


A network of interconnected devices that collect, exchange, and act on data from their environments. These "smart" devices (appliances, machinery) are embedded with sensors, software, and communication technologies.


IoT applications:


  • Homes - automation (smart thermostats, lighting)
  • Healthcare - patient monitoring (wearable devices)
  • Manufacturing - process streamlining (equipment monitoring, predictive maintenance)


Common Uses of the Internet


Communication:


  • Email - primary tool for communication
  • Chat rooms, newsgroups, listservs, internet-based calling


Research:


  • Business - market assessment
  • Students and writers - information gathering
  • Parents - finding information


Publishing:


  • World Wide Web (WWW) - multimedia content accessible 24/7
  • Websites - personal pages to corporate catalogues
  • Online business transactions, digital payments, cross-border commerce


Social Media


Interactive web applications and technologies that manage user-generated content. Facilitate sharing of information (messages, posts, comments, pictures, music, videos). Virtual communities of users with service-specific profiles.



Types of Social Media


Social Networking Sites:


  • Connect with friends, family, and brands.
  • Knowledge sharing and human-to-human interaction.
  • Sharing thoughts, content, photos, videos, forming groups, discussions.


Social Review Sites:


  • Display reviews from community members for locations and experiences.
  • Help with decision-making for booking restaurants, hotels, etc.


Image Sharing Sites:


  • Amplify the power of image sharing.
  • Share images, infographics, and illustrations.


Video Hosting Sites:


  • Revolutionized video watching, creating, and sharing.
  • Platforms optimized for streaming.


Community Blogs:


  • Provide a space for people to express their thoughts and connect with readers.


Discussion Sites:


  • Designed to spark conversations.
  • Attract people with shared interests and curiosities.


Forums:


  • Online platforms for discussions and community building.
